My argument for the pauline authorship of the pastoral epistles

May 23, 2024

As you all know, the Apostle Paul wrote a collection of letters now included in the New Testament, ranging from theological treatises to personal correspondence, which have significantly influenced Christian doctrine and practice. Out of all the letters attributed to Paul in the New Testament, skeptical critics like Bart Ehrman recognize only seven as genuinely his (Romans, 1 & 2 Corinthians, Galatians, Philippians, 1 Thessalonians, and Philemon). This presents a dilemma for Christians: either discard six books of their Bible or admit that the New Testament includes some well-intentioned forgeries.

Some New Testament scholars, like Bart Ehrman, argue that six of these letters are blatant forgeries, most notably 1 and 2 Timothy, which I'll be arguing against in this article. If we were to believe these scholars, it could put the Christian faith at great risk. In this short article, I'll be trying my best to argue FOR Pauline authorship. If the critics' arguments turn out to be weak, then it’s not the New Testament that loses credibility but its detractors.

Bart Ehrman's Main Arguments

In Ehrman's book, "Forged: Writing in the Name of God – Why the Bible's Authors Are Not Who We Think They Are," he lays out three main arguments as to why the Pastoral Epistles are forgeries. While there are more than three arguments, he clearly presents three as his main points.

Vocabulary Issues

Bart Ehrman’s first and arguably weakest point centers on vocabulary issues. This is a rather popular argument used by skeptics but is weak. Ehrman presents the argument the following way

“There are 848 different words used in the pastoral letters. Of that number 306-over one-third of them!-do not occur in any of the other Pauline letters of the New Testament. That’s an inordinately high number; especially given the fact that about two-thirds of these 306 words are used by Christian authors living in the second century. This suggests the author is using a vocabulary that was becoming more common after the days of Paul, and that he too, therefore, lived after Paul.” — Forged: Writing In The Name Of God – Why The Bible’s Authors Are Not Who We Think They Are. Pg 112

If you're not entirely convinced by this line of reasoning, I wholeheartly agree . We all have a tendency to adjust our linguistic repertoire based on the specific individuals we're conversing with. For instance, everyone reading this uses a different set of vocabulary when talking to different audiences. The Pastoral letters were Paul's personal letters directed to Timothy, his spiritual son. Of course, these will sound different compared to letters written to whole congregations of Christians. It's not difficult to discern the rationale behind the variation in his choice of words.

Ehrman even states that this isn't a strong argument oposing Pauline authorship. Quoting Bart: “Probably not too much stock should be placed in mere numbers. Everyone, after all, uses different words on different occasions, and most of us have a much richer stock of vocabulary than shows up in any given set of letters we write.”

Let's use a real life example to illustrate my rebutal of this point further. When you are talking to a co-worker you have known for years and years, you talk a certain way. But when writing an e-mail to address to whole company you obviously will use different language then when talking to a co-worker. I really don't think this argument is all that to strong, since differing vocabulary when talking to different people/groups is a well researched and common phenomenon.

The meaning of the word "Faith" in the Pastoral epistles

This is in my opinion Bart's biggest mistake in his takedown of the Pastoral Epistles. Here he just makes obvious Biblical mistakes. And I don't blame Bart for making these mistakes, but I find it sad that these arguments are just so widely accepted by the whole community of Biblical critics just because Bart came up with them, instead of digging deeper.

This argument from Bart is about the word "Faith", which is "pistes" in greek. The argument from Bart here isn't that faith is worthless and just a lack of evidence[1], which a lot of critics attribute to the word faith, but rather that the word is used in different ways in the Pastorals compared to the other books.

“In books such as Romans and Galatians faith refers to the trust a person has in Christ to bring about salvation through his death. In other words, the term describes a relationship with another; faith is a trust “in” Christ. The author of the Pastorals also uses the term “faith.” But here it is not about a relationship with Christ; faith now means the body of teaching that makes up the Christian religion. That is “the faith” (see Titus 1:13) Same word, different meaning.” –Forged, pp. 113

But hang on a second! That just isn’t true. Paul mostly does use the word ‘faith’ in the manner that Bart says, but he also does use it to refer to a body of doctrine at times in his undisputed letters. Here are some examples:

1 Corinthians 16:13 (ESV) “Be watchful, stand firm in the faith, act like men, be strong.”

2 Cor 13:5 “Examine yourselves, to see whether you are in the faith…

Gal 1:23 “They only were hearing it said, “He who used to persecute us is now preaching the faith he once tried to destroy.”

Phil 1:27 “Only let your manner of life be worthy of the gospel of Christ, so that whether I come and see you or am absent, I may hear of you that you are standing firm in one spirit, with one mind striving side by side for the faith of the gospel”

Ehrman’s just wrong here to suggest that Paul doesn’t use different shades of meaning when he’s using the word ‘faith’. He doesn’t use it in a wooden manner that has only one definition.

A Counter Argument: Polycarp and other early church fathers

If there was anyone in a position to know who wrote 1st and 2nd Timothy, it would have been the church father Polycarp. In his letter to the Philippian church written in about 110-120 AD, Polycarp quoted 1 Timothy 3:8, 6:7, 6:10 and 2 Timothy 2:12. He also mentions Paul by name four times in his letter, including some indications that he was familiar with the apostle’s martyrdom.

In an article from a Biblical Scholar Kenneth Berding. He he makes 2 big observations about Polycarp's usage of 1st and 2th Timothy.

For the sake of not bloating this article to much I won't be presenting the quotes from Kenneth Berding's article here, but please read them if you are interested.
The implication of the first observation is that Polycarp considers the phrases in each cluster to be Pauline. The implication of the second observation is that Polycarp considers the phrases which he quotes from 1 and 2 Timothy also to be from Paul.
This, of course, doesn’t prove that Polycarp is correct in his assessment. But… "Polycarp was doubtlessly the most significant ecclesiastical leader of the first half of II C. E.”

And around 40-50 years later, Irenaeus clearly attributes the Pastoral Letters to Paul. In his text Against Heresies, Irenaeus discusses heretics and quotes Paul's instruction to "avoid" after repeated warnings (Titus 3:10). Additionally, Irenaeus references Paul's advice to steer clear of individuals promoting "novelties of words of false knowledge" (1 Tim 6:20).

Moreover, the Didache's author, an early Christian writer from the late 1st century, directly references 1 Timothy 3:4. Additionally, the Pastoral Letters are cited by Clement of Alexandria (180 AD), Tertullian (220 AD), and Origen (230 AD). The early church's testimony is unequivocal: they regarded the Pastorals as authoritative and believed them to be genuinely penned by the Apostle Paul.

Main takeaways

In summary, the external evidence supporting both of these letters is robust, as is the internal evidence. Upon scrutiny, Bart's assertions about conflicting theologies in Paul's other letters appear unsubstantiated. There are compelling grounds to believe that Paul authored all the letters attributed to him by the early church, with insufficient evidence to suggest they are forgeries. Our New Testament includes 13 authentic letters from Paul.

Note [1], Not even one Biblical Greek scholar would ever agree that that is the meaning of the word pistes. And asserting that faith is just believe without evidence is characteristcally false.
